Unveiling the Vulnerabilities: The Crucial Need for Protecting Your Personal Data

Have you ever thought about the security of your personal data?

From the minute you open your online account, you download a video game, or you open a social media account, the security of your personal data might be compromised! Have you ever thought about who has access to your information—your gender, your age, your location, your bank account, or even your pictures? Even more, have you ever thought about your fundamental human right, “privacy”? What rights do Lebanese citizens enjoy when it comes to right to privacy and personal data protection.

December 10, 1948, the United Nations General Assembly proclaimed a human rights declaration in Paris. Article 12 of the declaration explicitly declares: “No one shall be subjected to arbitrary interference with his privacy, family, home, or correspondence, nor to attacks upon his honor and reputation. Everyone has the right to the protection of the law against such interference or attacks. 

Almost a century after, Lebanon’s legal framework regarding personal data protection remains weak. Starting with the Lebanese constitution being the Grundnorm according to Kelsen’s pyramid, it doesn’t ensure citizens' personal data privacy. Contrary to what many jurists consider, Articles 8 and 13 of the Lebanese Constitution to indirectly provide protection to communication, a report by Privacy International published january 29,2019 presented that this protection is not explicit.

October 2018, the Lebanese parliament passed Law Number 81 on Electronic Transactions and Personal Data, but till now, the absence of details and clear procedure poses a threat to  personal data and privacy prtection. The above law does not explicitly provide for the extraterritorial application of any data protection provisions and to date, Lebanon has no independent data protection authority that regulates or supervises the privacy of personal information. The authority is distributed between the Ministry of Economy when relating to the transfer of data and the Ministry of Interior with the Ministry of Defense when relating to the collection of data.

With this gap in the legal framework, isn't it essential to have an independent legal authority that protects personal data and privacy? And what tools and measures remain out there for you to ensure that your privacy and personal data is protected.
